A human story about PRT's FY26 route cuts — two comparable neighborhoods, different reliance on the bus, different cut treatments — and what ridership numbers can't say

Act I — The People
01
Opening

The City Before the Cut

~45 sec read
Proposed opening copy
"Every weekday morning, 200,000 people in Pittsburgh decide how to get where they need to go. Most of them don't think much about it. Some of them have no choice but to think about it every single day."
Sets stakes without vilifying anyone. Opens with universality before narrowing to specificity.
Bridging copy
"In 2025, Pittsburgh Regional Transit proposed eliminating 41 bus routes and reducing 54 others — touching every route in the system except two. The justification was efficiency. The reality is more complicated."
Motion direction
On page load: fade in headline over a static aerial or illustrated map of Pittsburgh. As user begins scrolling, the city "wakes up" — thin route lines draw themselves across the map, animated left-to-right over 2–3 seconds. No interaction yet. Purely cinematic. Route lines use muted gray — not yet colored by cut status.

Typographic treatment: headline in large serif, drop in from above with 600ms ease-out. Subhead fades in on delay.
Data shown in this scene
— Full route network GeoJSON (all active routes pre-cut)
— City boundary for context
— Aggregate stat: total_daily_riders (system-wide, FY25 baseline)

No cut data visible yet. This is the "before" world.
One scroll beat. The map builds, copy lands, user is oriented. No interaction. Forward momentum only.
02
Neighborhood Intro

Meet Two Neighborhoods

~90 sec read
Transition copy (before split screen)
"Behind every ridership number is a place — and the people who live there. Here are two neighborhoods the data says are worth comparing."
Signals that the pair is selected by analysis (similar scale / comparable route exposure where the data allows), not chosen for maximum drama. The contrast in transit reliance and in how the FY26 plan treats each place should emerge from the facts.
Neighborhood A — Lower Lawrenceville
Role in pair: Lower poverty (~14.5% below poverty line) · lower transit commute share among workers (~9.9%)
Population: 2,448 (2022 ACS)
Routes before cuts: 7 — 054, 064, 086, 087, 088, 091, 093
FY26 treatment: no routes eliminated; all seven see reductions (major frequency cuts and/or daily service ending at 11 p.m. per PRT methodology)
Human face: Composite Marcus (placeholder) — uses Butler St / Lawrenceville–Waterfront corridors for work and evenings out
Neighborhood B — Homewood South
Role in pair: Higher poverty (~36.3% below poverty line) · roughly twice the share of workers commuting by transit (~19.9% vs ~9.9%)
Population: 2,177 (2022 ACS)
Routes before cuts: 15 — includes 71D, 74, 77, 86, P1, P3, P68, P78, plus seven flyer routes slated for elimination
FY26 treatment: seven routes eliminated (P12, P16, P67, P69, P7, P71, P76) · eight remaining routes reduced (frequency / 11 p.m. span)
Human face: Composite Renee (placeholder) — relies on East Busway and connecting flyers for work and family trips
Neighborhood A — proposed intro copy (people first, same place)
"In Lower Lawrenceville, Marcus walks a few blocks to catch the 91 Butler Street or the 64 toward the Waterfront. Sometimes he takes the 93 toward Hazelwood. Driving is an option for him more days than not — but the bus is cheaper, and he doesn't have to hunt for parking when he meets friends on Penn Avenue."
Ground the scene in this place and these routes. ACS: ~58% of workers here commute by car/truck/van vs ~10% by transit — still label individuals as composites; do not imply "everyone drives."
Neighborhood B — proposed intro copy
"A short trip across the East End, Renee leaves Homewood South on the P1 or P3 East Busway, or catches the 74 toward Squirrel Hill. The P7 and P71 have been part of how her family reaches McKeesport and Swissvale. The bus isn't a backup plan — it's how the week stays on schedule."
Same narrative dignity as A; contrast is poverty + transit commute share + later elimination load, not moral framing. Several routes named here are slated for elimination under FY26 — the next scene makes that official.
Motion direction
Scroll triggers a split-screen reveal: left panel = Neighborhood A, right = Neighborhood B — same palette, equal visual weight (no "poor vs rich" lighting cues).

Highlight each neighborhood's own route bundle on its map chip (multiple thin lines acceptable). Colors: e.g. A routes in blue, B routes in warm rust — not the same routes on both sides. Labels should show route numbers / names that match routes_before for each place.

Optional micro-copy under each chip: "X routes before FY26" — no poverty or transit % yet.
Data shown in this scene
— Per neighborhood: neighborhood_name, routes_before, routes_before_count
— Stop-level map chips from route_stop_per_route / GTFS joined to hood
— transit_dependent_pct_proxy (or semantic commute-transit share) held back until Act III unless you want one subtle label — default: hold for Scene 06
— Pairing rules: n_shortlist.csv + homewood_south_vs_lower_lawrenceville_story_data.csv
— Editorial transparency: racial/ethnic composition differs sharply between these tracts (e.g. Black-alone share ~82% Lower Lawrenceville vs ~6% Homewood South in 2022 ACS — verify in story data CSV). The pair was chosen for poverty / transit / cut-type contrast, not to equate neighborhoods demographically. Say so in methodology or a footnote if race appears in copy.
Two scroll beats — one neighborhood, then the other. Slow, deliberate. The reader should feel two distinct places before they see how the plan treats each one differently.

Act II — The Cut
04
The Announcement

The Plan Treats Each Place Differently

~60 sec read
Proposed copy — the reveal
"In March 2025, Pittsburgh Regional Transit released its proposed FY26 service changes. For Lower Lawrenceville, no bus route serving the neighborhood was eliminated — but every one of its seven routes (54, 64, 86, 87, 88, 91, 93) would see cuts: mostly major frequency reductions, with 86 and 93 also ending daily service at 11 p.m. For Homewood South, the plan was starker: seven flyer routes vanished entirely — P12 Holiday Park, P16 Penn Hills, P67 Monroeville, P69 Trafford, P7 McKeesport, P71 Swissvale, and P76 Lincoln Highway — while eight remaining routes (including P1, P3, 71D, 74, 77, 86, P68, P78) would still run, but thinner and, in several cases, not past 11 p.m. Same proposal. Different routes. Different kinds of loss."
This is the pivot from "two places" to two treatments. The viewer should understand elimination vs reduction (and which routes) as official categories from PRT's methodology — cite primary_reduction_detail / step where appropriate. Do not imply the neighborhoods are identical on paper if route counts differ; label caveats in copy or a footnote.
Motion direction
Visual rupture on both panels, but asymmetric (match the real pair):

Lower Lawrenceville (A): no red X — all seven routes pulse amber only (frequency / span reduction). Optional label: "0 eliminated · 7 reduced."

Homewood South (B): seven lines (P12, P16, P67, P69, P7, P71, P76) get red X / ghost; eight others pulse amber. Hold side-by-side 1.5–2s; tooltips can show weekday ridership (e.g. summed ~5,200 baseline on eliminated set vs ~1,650 recent — from story data CSV).

Sound (optional): subtle, bureaucratic — not cinematic. Same as prior outline.

The transition should feel like two columns of a spreadsheet coming to life — precise, not melodramatic.
Data shown in this scene
— Pairing export: homewood_south_vs_lower_lawrenceville_story_data.csv (compare + per-route rows)
— Per neighborhood: routes_losing, reduced_routes, routes_after from fy26_route_n_profiles_all.csv
— Per affected route: route_status, primary_reduction_detail, primary_reduction_step from FY26_route_status_all.csv / prt_fy2026_route_cuts.csv
— Weekday ridership context for each highlighted route (for tooltip or small label — optional here, expanded in Scene 08)
— No citywide flood yet — still two neighborhoods only
Two beats: (1) Lower Lawrenceville (amber-only), (2) Homewood South (red + amber). Keep copy tight; scope explosion is Scene 05.

Act III — Treatment, Access & Pattern
06
Impact Comparison

Different Cuts. Different Capacity to Absorb Them.

~120 sec read
Transition back to the two places
"Back to Lower Lawrenceville and Homewood South. The plan already treated them differently on the map — seven full removals in Homewood, none in Lawrenceville. What that feels like on the ground also depends on what else is available, and on how deeply the neighborhood relies on the bus."
Lower Lawrenceville — after its cuts
Treatment recap: 0 eliminated · 7 reduced (54, 64, 87, 88, 91: major frequency reduction per PRT step 6; 86, 93: also end at 11 p.m. nightly, step 4)
"Marcus still has the same seven route numbers on the map — but waits are longer, last buses earlier, and the 'same' trip may no longer be viable for a late shift. Driving is a fallback the ACS suggests is common here (~58% of workers commute by car/truck/van vs ~10% by transit — but many households still depend on the bus)."

Impact band: Inconvenience · longer waits · earlier end of service — no entire corridors removed.
Alternatives: map all seven surviving lines from GTFS; compare headways pre/post where schedule data exists.
Homewood South — after its cuts
Treatment recap: 7 eliminated (P12, P16, P67, P69, P7, P71, P76) · 8 reduced (P1, P3, 71D, 74, 77, 86, P68, P78 — frequency and/or 11 p.m. span)
"Renee loses whole one-seat rides to Swissvale, McKeesport, Monroeville, Trafford, Penn Hills, Holiday Park, and the Lincoln Highway corridor. She can still reach downtown on the Busway — but many east-suburban links are gone or require a longer walk to a thinner route."

Impact band: Network geometry changes, not only headways — verify with isochrones / transfer tables before claiming job loss.
Alternatives: map remaining eight routes + honest transfer paths; note P1/P3 still carry heavy baseline ridership (~10.4k + ~2.8k weekday system averages pre-COVID on those lines alone).
Proposed copy — the divergence statement
"Ridership data can show which lines carried fewer people. It does not, by itself, show which neighborhoods had the least room to absorb a loss — or how elimination versus reduction lands when you are already depending on the bus."
Thesis stated plainly after the viewer has seen two different cut profiles. Keeps PRT's efficiency logic in play without pretending one route tied both places together.
Motion direction
Return to split-screen. Each side uses its own post-cut network (not the same alternative geometry copy-pasted).

Neighborhood A: animated dotted paths to nearest surviving stops / routes; labels with walk minutes. If driving is the realistic dominant shift for the composite story, a second beat can show that as text-only (no caricature).

Neighborhood B: same visual language — but paths may be longer, fewer, or dead-end at gaps; optional comparison of headway or span loss on remaining routes (if data supports).

Below each panel: thin demographic strips on scroll — Below poverty line: ~14.5% Lawrenceville vs ~36.3% Homewood · Workers using public transit: ~9.9% vs ~19.9% · Car/truck/van commute: ~58% vs ~70% (ACS 2022, resident workers — not riders-only). Same typography both sides.
Data shown in this scene
— Post-cut alternative access: walking distance to nearest remaining service per neighborhood centroid or representative point (method documented)
— share_commute_public_transit or project proxy transit_dependent_pct_proxy + poverty fields from n_profiles_new.csv / fy26_route_n_profiles_all.csv
— Vehicle access: derive carefully from ACS (e.g. workers by commute mode; no-car household share if computed)
— Route-level treatment flags for each corridor discussed
— Source: ACS + GTFS / route-stop-table.csv · compare rows in homewood_south_vs_lower_lawrenceville_story_data.csv
Emotional core. Four beats: (1) Lawrenceville post-cut (same routes, worse service), (2) Homewood post-cut (seven corridors gone), (3) demographic strips for both, (4) thesis copy. Do not compress — this scene carries the equity argument for the two-place spine.

Act IV — The Justification & Return
08
PRT's Perspective

Why PRT Says the Cuts Are Necessary

~90 sec read
Section framing copy — fairness statement
"Pittsburgh Regional Transit did not make these decisions without reason. Here is what the data shows — and here is what PRT has said publicly about why."
Critical: this scene must be presented without sarcasm or framing that pre-judges the conclusion. PRT's justification deserves full and fair presentation. The story's critique comes from the data, not the narration.
Ridership context copy
"PRT ridership has not recovered to pre-pandemic levels. System-wide, daily boardings are [X]% below 2019 numbers. Routes proposed for elimination average [Y] daily riders — compared to a system-wide average of [Z]."
Motion direction — ridership chart
Line chart: system-wide ridership 2018–2025. The 2020 drop is visible and dramatic. The partial recovery. The plateaued "new normal."

On scroll, a second overlay draws in: ridership lines for Homewood's seven eliminated flyers plus a sample of Lawrenceville's seven reduced locals (or system-wide eliminated average). Their decline is visible and real — this is not fabricated justification.

Chart style: clean, editorial. No gridlines. Annotated with two or three key moments (pandemic start, reopening, current).

The visual should be honest. PRT is not wrong about ridership declining. The story's question is whether ridership decline is the only lens that should matter.
PRT direct quote / justification copy
"[Direct quote from PRT CEO, board materials, or public statement explaining the ridership-based decision framework — TBD, source from official docs]"
Must be a direct, verified quote. Do not paraphrase PRT's position — quote it. Attribution required.
Complicating copy — the pivot
"Ridership numbers measure who is using the system. They do not measure who would have no alternative if they couldn't — or how much harder a full elimination is to absorb than a frequency trim."
This is the pivot back toward the human story. One sentence. No editorializing beyond this — the data and the two neighborhoods carry the rest.
Data shown in this scene
— Ridership time series: year, month, system_boardings (2018–2025)
— Ridership for featured routes (both places) vs. system average: route_id, avg_daily_riders, system_avg_daily_riders
— Cost-per-rider for eliminated routes vs. system average (if available)
— Official PRT source: FY26 budget proposal, board meeting minutes, or press statement
This scene requires the most editorial care. Do not rush it. Two scroll beats: (1) chart builds, (2) PRT quote + pivot. The pivot sentence should arrive alone — white space above and below it.

CTA — The Question
10
Closing

Is This Equitable?

~60 sec read
Return to the two neighborhoods — brief
"In Lower Lawrenceville, Marcus is re-timing his week around longer waits and earlier last buses — frustrated, but still on the same lines.

In Homewood South, Renee is re-drawing her map: seven familiar routes won't be there at all."
Short. Asymmetric on purpose if the data-supported impacts diverge. The gap between the two sentences should reflect treatment + context, not moral judgment.
Closing copy — the question
"Pittsburgh Regional Transit's FY26 proposal was built on one question: how many people are using each route?

But there is another question — one that the ridership data cannot answer.

When we decide how to allocate a public system, do we measure efficiency — or do we measure need?"
We do not answer this. We do not suggest an answer. We leave the viewer with the question. The CTA below invites action — but we do not tell them what to think.
CTA interactions required
Three equal-weight call-to-action options (no hierarchy — we are not steering):

1. Attend a public hearing — links to PRT meeting schedule (verify current URL)
2. Submit a public comment — links to PRT comment form or email
3. Share this story — native share, copy link

Optional fourth: Read the full FY26 proposal — link to official PRT budget document

Design: minimal. Three text links or ghost buttons. No urgency language, no petition framing. Just: here are ways to participate.
Motion direction — closing
The city map from Scene 01 returns — but now routes are colored by cut status, as the viewer last saw them. Full circle. The city that opened the story is not the same city that closes it.

The question ("Is this equitable?") should be the last large typographic element the reader sees — set in the display serif, large, centered, plenty of white space above it. It should feel like it's still ringing after the reader stops scrolling.

No autoplay. No countdown. The reader leaves when they're ready.
Data shown in this scene
— Full cut map (same as Scene 05, returning as a visual echo)
— Links: PRT public comment page, PRT board meeting schedule, FY26 budget doc URL
— Verify all URLs are current before launch
The question is the last copy element. Below it: white space, the map, CTA links. No more narration after the question. The reader is in the driver's seat now.

Open Editorial Questions
1. Composite names & vignettes. Names should feel specific and regional (Pittsburgh-native), not generic. Consult community voices or organizations in the two featured neighborhoods for authenticity review — composites must not be mistaken for real individuals.
2. Featured pair locked. Scenes 02–07 use Lower Lawrenceville vs Homewood South — see data/homewood_south_vs_lower_lawrenceville_story_data.csv. Racial composition is not matched between tracts; poverty, transit commute share, and elimination count are the intentional contrasts. Review composite copy with community input.
3. PRT quote sourcing. Scene 08 requires a direct, attributed quote from PRT's official communications. Source from: FY26 budget document, board meeting transcripts, PRT CEO press statements, or WESA/PGH Post-Gazette coverage. No paraphrase.
4. Public comment quotes (Scene 03). Requires sourced real-world testimony. PRT public hearing transcripts are the primary source. If not publicly available, WESA and Pittsburgh Post-Gazette coverage of hearings often includes direct rider quotes.
5. "Is this equitable?" — framing. This question must be the last word. Resist any instinct in the final CTA copy to nudge toward an answer. If the story has done its work, the reader already knows what they think.
